Privacy & Civil Liberties Engineering

(Palantir Explained, #5)

Published: 26 January 2022

Editor’s note: This is the fifth post in Palantir Explained, a series that explores a range of topics, including our approach to privacy, security, AI/ML safety and more. Previous posts explored trust in data, our business model, purpose-based access controls, and data anonymization.

Protecting privacy and upholding liberal democratic values have been central to Palantir’s identity and mission since our founding in 2003; and we have intentionally adopted a business model that allows us to follow through with this commitment. We make software that enables the institutions that serve our societies to use their data responsibly and effectively, and we do not trade in personal data at all.

Many of the most important institutions in the world rely on Palantir software to support critical and sensitive operations. These organizations have exceptionally high security and data protection standards — for reasons ranging from protecting national security to complying with the world’s most stringent privacy laws — and they would not trust our software if it did not meet and exceed these standards.

Achieving such a strong security record — and empowering institutions to protect people’s data in the process — was necessary for our business to succeed. For example, our earliest customers in national security required very fine-grained control over who could see what data, granting permissions only on a precise need-to-know basis. This functionality can equally enhance privacy when applied to other domains.

We realized early on that effectively creating privacy-protective technology and business practices required a dedicated focus, a significant investment, and a broad mandate. This is why Palantir created one of the world’s first Privacy & Civil Liberties Engineering (“PCL”) teams more than a decade ago, which continues to be an essential part of the company’s identity and work.

Rejecting Unacceptable Tradeoffs

Poorly conceived technologies present false tradeoffs. One argument that we have long rejected is that national security requires sacrificing citizens’ privacy and civil liberties. Our experience has been that through the thoughtful and principled development of technology to support institutions, we can harmoniously advance these and other rights. It is essential, for example, to treat fairness as a first order concern when designing systems to support decision making.

Ultimately, Palantir’s work is unjustifiable if it does not strengthen privacy and uphold liberal democratic values. Citizens would rightly oppose it, and we would be undermining the very societies and convictions that we seek to defend. Thus a motivating question for every engineer in the company is “Do I want to live in the kind of world that the technology we’re building would enable?”

To help our engineers and leadership achieve this mission and navigate the difficult questions that arise, Palantir formed the Privacy & Civil Liberties Engineering (“PCL”) team as a specialist vanguard to advise and lead on these issues.

The Privacy & Civil Liberties Engineering Team

The goal of the PCL team is to design, build, and deploy privacy-protective technologies, and to foster a culture of responsibility around their development and use.

The inclusion of the term “engineering” in this title is a deliberate one. Policy is essential, and our first obligation is always to follow policy as defined by public officials; but relying on policy alone to ensure ethical practice provides limited confidence that others can or will always adopt it. Similarly, compliance teams with a purely legalistic approach may fail to anticipate new issues that technological development presents.

“Engineering” is a core responsibility of the PCL team in order to ensure that our values are encoded into our software at a fundamental level, and to ensure that the team is exposed to exactly the same issues as every other engineer in the company. This gives the PCL team a nuanced understanding of frontline issues, forcing us to be solution-oriented rather than being focused solely on academic theory with limited attachment to real-world tensions.

As with policy and compliance, technical controls alone cannot avert negative societal outcomes, which are fundamentally human problems, and may arise in subtle, complex, and context-dependent ways. Navigating this interplay requires a diverse and multidisciplinary team, and so in addition to engineers, the team comprises lawyers, data scientists, policy specialists, social scientists, and philosophers, all of whom closely collaborate in the pursuit of holistic and thoughtful software development and deployment.

We continually aspire to produce software according to the principle of “privacy by design and default.” This means that the standard configuration of the software is maximally privacy protective: any access that a user gets to personal data must be explicitly and deliberately permitted by the responsible organization; personal data should be deleted once its retention is no longer justified; data is displayed at the minimum necessary sensitivity; all user actions are auditable to ensure they act responsibly, and so forth.

The team also contributes directly to product development, including developing “privacy-enhancing technologies” that can be deployed on top of default protections, supporting sophisticated encryption, justification, retention, and data-tracking approaches to meet a great range of compliance contexts and ethical imperatives. These tools enable our customers to meet — and often exceed — the most rigorous data handling standards; and we strive to give them the tools to set the bar for privacy expectations ever higher.

Privacy Alone is Insufficient

When making software for processing data, ethical engineering isn’t just about about privacy and data protection. Data is used as information about the world, often to make consequential decisions that will greatly affect people’s lives. The effects of these decisions vary across workflows and domains, as do the ethical concerns that must factor into them.

While the PCL team works to foster a range of experience and expertise in order to respect key principles and to help our customers navigate these questions, ultimately the question of how data can be most responsibly used in all these contexts often requires us to engage directly with those who can supplement and enrich our perspectives.

Thus, an essential second responsibility for the team is to act as interface between other subject-matter experts — from civil society, academic centers, or policymaking centers — and our engineers and customers. We continuously consult with these experts in order to ensure we are providing the most responsible, thoughtful, and effective guidance to our engineers and the organizations our products support.

This means organizing lectures, discussions, and trainings within the company. It means meeting and consulting with a variety of community members and stakeholders — including those who may be critical of aspects of our work. It means participating in conferences, and ultimately being more transparent, including publishing blog posts, videos, and books about our work. We must keep working to set this transparency and engagement bar higher, and seek new ways of fulfilling this obligation.

In 2012, we formed the Palantir Council of Advisors on Privacy & Civil Liberties (“PCAP”). These advisors are world-renowned experts representing a diversity of perspectives and deep expertise in legal practice, policy, technical, administrative, and other facets of the field. It includes a former Australia Privacy Commissioner, a founder of the prominent UK advocacy organization Big Brother Watch, a former US Department of Justice Chief Privacy Officer, an Engineering Chair in the Department of Informatics at Technische Universität München, a former White House Deputy Chief Technology Officer for Internet Policy, and a number of other foundational scholars and lawyers in the privacy legal field.

All of this reflects a fundamental conviction of the company, strengthened by long experience: technology cannot solve all the world’s problems. The introduction of technologies into real world contexts can generate diverse and sometimes difficult-to-anticipate effects; responsible technological design must be infused with a respect for the complexity of this undertaking.
