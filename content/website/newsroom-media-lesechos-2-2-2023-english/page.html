Interview with Les Echos | Palantir CEO Alex Karp

Published 2nd February 2023

Terrorist attacks, war in Ukraine, pandemic, enterprise data management...In an interview with ‘Les Echos’, Palantir CEO Alex Karp explains why he believes his products can change everything for his public and private customers. He places the destiny of his company in the context of the confrontation between China and the West.

How is Palantir doing? How fast is the company growing?

American financiers have a “positivist” philosophical vision: they measure the performance of the company according to its sales forecasts. This is relevant, unless the business in question changes the world in which these predictions are made. But in my humble opinion, this is the case with our platform. Without our product, there would be multiple terrorist attacks that would bring the extreme right to power. I think that it has enormous value.

To spin the philosophical metaphor, I would say that this is the first reason to apply a “Heideggerian” approach to evaluate Palantir. The second “Heideggerian" point, is that Palantir has, at least in the United Kingdom and the United States, changed the trajectory of the pandemic by organizing the distribution of equipment (masks, oxygen…) and vaccines. I tell my friends on the left that of the million people we rescued, many were poor, obese or had co-morbidities. And I can't say anything, but Palantir played a crucial role in Ukraine. That being said, most of our business is with the US government. Growing sharply, 35% at an average annual rate. As for our activities with commercial enterprises, they are growing at a crazy rate.

What is special about the Palantir platform?

Most software companies do powerpoint presentations. Our products really interact with data and change the way businesses are working. Companies have the choice between spending $1 billion to get started or to take Palantir for $1.5 million a year on average. Our first product (“Gotham”), for the police, for example, retrieves the relevant data — legally available, of course — and uses it to identify terrorists.

Our Foundry product, for commercial enterprises, identifies the sensitive sensors according to business needs, standardizes the data and changes the way to fly a plane or explore underground for oil, for example. In one or two months, we change the way of operating, and this is across the entire company. We work ahead, our product was designed to help solve the problems associated with a pandemic before it happened. Palantir is well funded for this, but above all we have the ideas to anticipate. We are like Picasso and Matisse: when they started to paint, people said: "this is worthless, who's going to buy this?"

What is France for Palantir?

Palantir has great customers in France, such as Airbus, which is a large share, and masks the growth we are achieving with other companies. But if we have 200 people here, it's for the mathematical acuity and the aesthetic sensibility of the French. I'm in Paris for the talent! I find the nicest and most talented people here, something the rest of Silicon Valley still neglects. America is far ahead when it comes to technology but France has a good tech ecosystem.

Why did Palantir leave Silicon Valley and move to Denver?

Silicon Valley is very good at exporting societally-harmful products. I am not Jesus, but the end goal of Palantir is to do good — to improve the world.

What are you doing against the image you have of being an arm of the CIA?

This is obviously not true. The CIA invested $1 million initially through its incubation fund. But I would say that this mistaken image is more beneficial to us than anything else. We hire people who at 22 can have the responsibilities of someone fifty years old. If they are unable to see for themselves that we are not a window into the CIA — I have no reason to trust them, it’s a test.

Of course this image prevents us from recruiting certain clients, but only those that would be here for the short term anyways.

What do you say to those who think you can no longer work for the French DGSI?

With Palantir, the data does not go to the United States. No individual has access to it. We don't know what the French government is doing. There is nothing that would mean losing sovereignty over it. If France finds better than Palantir, Hallelujah. As for us, I am convinced that we are helping the country. We are not in it for the money with this low-paying contract.

David Barroux, Nicolas Madelaine, Leïla Marchand – 2 February 2023
