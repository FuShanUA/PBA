Palantir Technologies’ Approach to AI Ethics

Palantir’s approach to AI Ethics is constructed upon a more expansive recognition that our technology — i.e., the software platforms we build and deploy to our customers — does not exist in vacuum, but rather is inextricably tied to its contexts of application, its operational uses, and the full data operating environment that surrounds the much narrower AI components that tend to dominate the discourse of AI Ethics.

We assert an ethics of technology that applies to the full contexts of its use. These contexts each implicate their own situated set of domain-specific demands, functional expectations, and ethical obligations. This framing compels us to put AI in its appropriate place: as a tool among other tools of varying sophistication and inexorably embedded in a world of tangible actions and consequences.

Revised: April, 2025

We are committed to the following principles for advancing this approach:

01 /

Focus on the fully integrated system, not just its component tools.

Technology ethics that center AI/ML as the locus of moral inquiry have lost the forest for the trees. Applied ethical considerations are almost never localized to a specific algorithmic output, for example, and instead run the length of the system, including its infrastructure, data foundation, and user interfaces.

Given the broad application and significant power that rests in data, a trustworthy and secure data foundation is required. This should include (but is not limited to) high-quality data integration, pipeline management, data quality checks, system audit logs, granular access controls, the ability to version and branch data, and collaboration features to annotate datasets and identify addressable issues over time (e.g., statistical and other forms of unwanted data bias).

02 /

Acknowledge technology’s limits.

Palantir believes that any potential application of AI must begin with a sober assessment of what the technology can and cannot do, and (perhaps more critically) what it should and should not be permitted to do.

When we as a company contemplate implementing machine-assisted or fully-automated decision-making processes through the use of machine learning or artificial intelligence, we bear in mind the technology's inherent limitations. Foremost, AI applications encode operations and processes. As such, these tools are nothing more than mathematical, statistical, or programmatic formalisms. This is not the same as thinking, understanding or making moral judgements.

03 /

Don't solve problems that shouldn't be solved.

There are certain problems that simply should not avail themselves to AI interventions, for a number of reasons. Some problem types are ill-suited for AI/ML approaches because they violate legal and community norms. For example, research applying facial recognition technology to predict sexual orientation from facial features [1] implicates sensitive category inferences that are morally, scientifically, and legally questionable.

Where proposed technology interventions involve complex social behavior, potentially negative feedback loops, and subtly compounding disparate impacts, researchers and technologists should give pause to consider whether adequate mitigation measures can be instituted to rein in risks.

Furthermore, we believe that technology companies and their institutional counterparts have a responsibility to evaluate, as a threshold matter, whether mathematical optimization is simply ill-equipped to capture fundamentally irreducible qualitative phenomena carrying significant societal impacts. Recognizing that once the genie is out of the bottle, it may be inordinately difficult to put it back, our approach to AI entails a threshold evaluation of whether the application at hand should even be initiated (irrespective of whether it may be technically feasible).

04 /

Adhere to methodological best practices for sound data science.

The developing data science, machine learning, and domain-specific literature provide critical methodological guidance for identifying and addressing challenges in data modeling, use, and governance. The literature should be consulted regularly by practitioners and to the greatest extent possible, commonly accepted best practices should be embedded into capabilities and workflows. These methodical considerations include (but are not necessarily limited to):

[a] Use Features Responsibly

We believe that context matters and is essential to determining whether specific types of information should be used in AI. For example, it is one thing to seek to analyze whether sensitive categories such as race or gender linked to genetic attributes correlate with certain disease prevalences. It is quite another thing to seek an association between those same immutable personal attributes and one’s likelihood to default on a loan. Where AI seeks to model (and predict) outcomes with real impact on peoples’ lives but with no ostensible or causally justifiable association with their sensitive personal attributes (e.g., race, gender, ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, age, family status, etc.), an active effort may be required to minimize model reliance on related features, in the form of explicit sensitive data categories, as well as implicit or proxy features.[2] Where such sensitive categories are implicated in programs that use AI either by necessity or choice, their inclusion should be identified and, where appropriate, contextually justified, in addition to complying with any formal requirements under applicable law.

[b] Methodically Assess and Address Bias

Any credible AI effort must grapple with the “garbage-in-garbage-out” problem. Stated differently, no matter how rigorous the design of an AI application is, if the data used for training and development is irredeemably flawed, the system output will almost certainly be compromised as well. This issue is even more pronounced where training data reflects systemic or other institutionalized forms of bias. A recent industry example of this issue involved the attempted use of ML to optimize talent recruitment.[3] However, because historical hiring practices biased towards male candidates, the model “learned” to perpetuate a similar bias. We believe that the implementation of any AI system requires a clear assessment of the fidelity, quality, and representativeness of the data upon which its models are built and trained. These evaluations must seek to understand how biases in data collection and aggregation may encode and perpetuate biased practices in the real world. If, for example, population groups are systemically under- or over-represented by virtue of biased or discriminatory data gathering practices, suitable techniques must be employed to de-bias the data through means such as re-sampling, collecting new data, applying adjusted weight classes, or modifying the algorithm itself to explicitly nullify or minimize the known bias. At minimum, where such biases exist, they should be studied, understood, and appropriately documented so that decision makers can, with full awareness, make competent evaluations of the trade-offs incurred by accepting some measure of bias (which may be legitimate or acceptable in specific contexts) in exchange for optimizing on other evaluation parameters.

[c] Carefully Monitor Outcomes, Understand Fairness Assessment Trade-offs

An ethical approach to AI must also deal with the risks associated with disproportionate impacts in that system's empirical outcomes. Evaluating how AI outcomes may be skewed across different population segments, including groups representing sensitive or vulnerable categories (e.g., age, gender, race, ethnicity), can assist in further algorithmic refinements aimed at optimizing outcomes on appropriate evaluation parameters. One particular challenge in this regard, however, is that societal notions of fairness may not always cleanly translate into mathematical correctness in the form of specific optimization parameters such as (in the context of binary classifier identification) True Positive, False Positive, Positive Predictive Value, False Discovery Rate, or any of an expansive slew of potential performance evaluation measures. Programs using AI that seek to encode a single canonical measure for evaluating and assessing determinations of model fairness must grapple with both this normative ambiguity (i.e., that there may be irreducible qualitative aspects of fairness) and also the demonstrable mathematical impossibility of being able to achieve all (or even most) measures of fairness at all times [4]. We believe that programs using AI that seek to produce ethically defensible outcomes must forthrightly evaluate and understand these trade-offs. Ultimately, an AI program should be intentional and explicit in determining which optimization measures are employed and documenting the consequences of those decisions.

[d] Ensure Auditability, Explainability, and Interpretability

We believe that ethical AI uses must enforce accountability through a number of discrete measures and techniques. Auditing in the form of in-process system records and audit logs should be employed where practicable to ensure that processing events contributing to specific outcomes can, at later stages, be interrogated and documented as needed. AI models should to the extent possible also be explainable to various relevant stakeholders, subject to regulatory, legal, and normative expectations. For program supervisors and government regulators, explanations of the underlying mechanics and decision-making logic of the system must be presentable in reasonably accessible terms, which may include documentation of which features a model uses, how those features are weighted, and how the algorithm decides what is important and what is not. These explanations should ideally be provided in terms that are accessible to a reasonably competent lay audience (i.e., in terms that do not require a graduate degree in artificial intelligence, machine learning, and adjacent fields to understand). Finally, interpretations of models should, in alignment with the preceding “Carefully Monitor Outcomes, Understand Fairness Assessment Trade-offs” principle, provide a clear assessment and trade-offs made and attendant consequences of choosing certain optimization parameters (such as a particular Fairness measure) over others.

05 /

Keep AI responsible, accountable, and oriented towards humans.

Generally speaking, AI is both most effective and most defensible when employed to assist and enhance human execution and decision making rather than replacing it.

Particularly in applications that carry significant impacts on individuals' livelihoods and well-being, the limits of AI must be acknowledged as a heuristic for determining the right level of human intervention to ensure moral agency and culpability. As should be the case with all technologies, the impact of AI should be in elevating humanity, not in undermining, endangering, or replacing it.

06 /

Promote multi-stakeholder engagement.

A critical operational principle for ethical data usage — for programs that use AI and more generally — is broad stakeholder involvement in building, deploying, and overseeing the data resources and tools, as well as input in specific research selection and visibility into research outcomes. Multi-stakeholder engagement is critical to validating any data-focused initiative and will ultimately build trust across the community of interested and affected parties. This includes constructing and configuring systems interfaces that support and enable end-users to build upon, augment, and enhance their subject matter expertise in their interactions and applications of systems that use AI.

However, incentivizing multi-stakeholder engagement also requires trust and the guarantee that partners can maintain a level of security and control over their data to ensure proper use. A technical infrastructure capable of enabling multi-stakeholder engagement could include, but is not limited to: technical infrastructure for secure data enclaves, collaboration and access control tools, dataset visualizations, automated compliance reviews, and visual results modeling to promote understanding across technical and non-technical stakeholders.

Additionally, external oversight authorities such as relevant regulatory authorities should also be engaged at early stages to provide topical insights and thereby mitigate risks of adverse regulatory actions. Context-permitting, institutions deploying programs that use AI should consider engaging with the public and other community stakeholders, such as advocacy and civil society groups, as early as possible. This early community engagement can help to demystify the intended applications, avoid unwarranted suspicions, provide an opportunity for stakeholders to voice their concerns, and enable pathways for exploring whether and how those concerns may be addressed.

07 /

Ensure technical, governance, and cultural awareness in data and technology applications.

Data best practices around Privacy and Civil Liberties (PCL), bias, and ethics should be reinforced by technical capabilities (e.g., stringent security and access controls) through a combination of technical infrastructure that facilitates enforcement of key data protection best practices; governance that incentivizes and rewards best practices; and cultural awareness and discipline-specific frameworks to contextualize the appropriateness of data and data-driven technologies.

Recognizing the complex ethical, social, policy, and legal ramifications of evolving data landscapes and data processing techniques, Palantir employs a team of dedicated privacy and civil liberties (PCL) experts across the fields of law, engineering, data protection, mathematics, computer science, social science, and philosophy to help ensure a culture that fosters the responsible development and deployment of technologies.
