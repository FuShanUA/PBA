Editor’s Note: Author Andrew Girvin is the head of biomedical R&D at Palantir.

I joined Palantir in 2010 as one of the first employees focusing on healthcare and biomedical R&D. Last month, we held an event that brought several of our partners across the health space together for the first time. The progress I heard about at HealthCon 2019 inspired me to reflect on the challenge that brought me to Palantir and the progress made in addressing it.

2010: The general problem

In the summer of 2010, I’d just finished my Ph.D. in immunology at Stanford, and I was eager to find my next opportunity.

Biology was moving from the deep exploration of individual genes towards high-throughput methods that could simultaneously interrogate tens of thousands of genes. This wealth of data created myriad new opportunities, along with significant challenges. When a single assay began producing hundreds of interesting hypotheses about genes, proteins, microRNAs, or metabolites, it became really difficult to prioritize follow up experiments.

In order to understand the results of a single experiment, a biologist had to get context from a multitude of their institution’s internal data sets in addition to siloed public sources like HGNC, uniprot, StringDB, reactome, msigdb, KEGG — the list goes on. Each data set used a different ID, and a different data format. It had become prohibitively difficult to make decisions using all the available data.

Every week, a new, powerful bioinformatics approach was published, but the techniques were hard to share and reproduce, and inaccessible to the biologists with the deepest subject matter expertise in a given field. The gulf between those who could analyze the results of an experiment and those who could interpret these results was quickly widening. In some cases, user-friendly tools did exist, but they were built for specific data types, and specific questions, and were not sufficiently flexible to handle the explosion of new omics techniques.

The gulf between those who could analyze the results of an experiment and those who could interpret these results was quickly widening.

Fortunately, I was living in Palo Alto and found my way to Palantir, where I met a team of computer scientists working to solve the general problem of bringing data together from many sources, harmonizing it, mapping it to an ontology, and making it useful for deep subject-matter experts.

I knew I was taking a risk in joining Palantir, with just a small fraction of its engineering team dedicated to the health space at the time. But I also knew that this kind of problem could never be solved with one-off efforts. Only a large team of top engineers tackling the general problem could build a solution flexible enough to handle all the data modalities that exist today and those that would be invented tomorrow.

2019: “Data as the substrate”

Fast forward to 2019: in March, Palantir hosted our first annual HealthCon to explore the breakdown of silos at the intersection of health, data, and technology. We were joined by leaders from cancer centers, pharma companies, payers, providers, and the federal health community, including FDA, NIH, CDC, and PEPFAR. Hearing the speakers on the HealthCon stage reminded me of just how far we’ve come since 2010.

In his remarks, Dr. Sean Khozin, Associate Director of the Oncology Center of Excellence, and Director at FDA INFORMED, reinforced our notion of this “general problem” of data integration. “We’ve traditionally thought about medicine through specialization,” said Dr. Khozin. “Across specialties, there was always an accumulation of information that exceeded the ability for one person to learn all the facts. Data and organizational silos were required to make progress and to enable us to become subject matter experts. We’ve picked the low-hanging fruit — hygiene, vaccinations.”

“But now,” he continued, “we’re looking at the next generation — how we think about and treat the patient as an individual. We must break down the silos that initially got us here, with data being the substrate.”

Of course, customizing treatment strategies to the specific biology of an individual patient, or personalized medicine, isn’t a new idea. Since the Human Genome Project was announced almost two decades ago, there have been countless analyses of the relationship between genomic characteristics of patients and their treatment outcomes, but the development of concrete clinical applications of this work has been slower than we had hoped. For example, the latest immuno-oncology drugs produce exciting outcomes, but only for a minority of patients, and in most cases, we have no diagnostic that can effectively predict drug response from the genomic features of the patient or tumor.

This makes it challenging for physicians to use this data to select the best therapy for their patients, and for payers and regulators to evaluate the efficacy of new medicines across diverse patient populations.

Despite these challenges, conference participants were optimistic about the future. With the cost of whole genome sequencing dropping exponentially, and the appearance of dozens of new ‘omics platforms over the last decade, we are beginning to generate datasets with sufficient statistical power to uncover reliable genomic correlates of drug response. For example, in 2017, the FDA approved the first oncology drug for use by any patient whose tumor has a particular genomic feature (biomarker), regardless of its tissue of origin. We are beginning to move beyond crude, anatomical classifications of disease to precise, molecular, and mechanistic definitions.

We are beginning to generate datasets with sufficient statistical power to uncover reliable genomic correlates of drug response.

The ‘omics revolution has created powerful new data, but it has also significantly exacerbated the challenges faced by researchers as they attempt to ask questions across all the siloed information relevant to a patient population. In other words, personalized medicine is just one of the latest instances of the problem that brought me to Palantir nine years ago.

Since then, we’ve developed technology to securely integrate highly complex data from many sources, in many formats, and allow clinicians and scientists to make sense of it in a meaningful way. By applying these new tools to make sense of the massive growth in genomic data, we can see meaningful advancement towards personalized medicine.

Shared momentum

HealthCon speakers covered topics from personalized medicine to early R&D to real world evidence (RWE) analysis. I was struck by both the breadth of progress and the consistency of the underlying data integration challenge. Solving the general problem is driving results across the drug discovery value chain, and it was rewarding to hear our partners discuss how they’re using our software to consistently advance against these challenges.

Accelerating drug discovery

Representing the earliest stages of the pharmaceutical value chain, scientists at the National Institutes of Health (both the National Cancer Institute or NCI and the National Center for Accelerating Translational Science, or NCATS), discussed the research outcomes achieved through a shared commitment to experimentation and dismantling silos.

“The biggest shift we’ve made is a cultural one, to actually have a space where it’s safe to experiment,” said Sam Michael, CIO of NCATS, onstage. “We are trying to break these silos down, both within our intramural program, in our own research, but also extramural.”

NIH scientists discussed how they used Palantir Foundry to integrate and analyze high-throughput screening, whole-genome sequencing, proteomic, transcriptomic, copy-number variation, and clinical data from a variety of sources to better understand molecular factors that influence drug response. One group discussed the application of machine learning techniques across the full history of screening results to surface potential repurposing opportunities. NIH scientists also exhibited a poster that described how Foundry drove the prediction and successful experimental validation of novel synergistic drug combinations for an oncology indication.

Understanding drug performance in clinical trials and medical practice

HealthCon included a poster session where medical officers at the FDA described how they used their instance of Palantir to integrate anonymized, patient-level data across thousands of clinical trials that they have reviewed. By pooling this data, the FDA can identify trends that may not be significant in individual trials alone. In one case, the medical officers identified new safety risks associated with immuno-oncology drugs (Amiri-Kordestani et al, 2018); in another, researchers evaluated the benefits and risks of CDKIs for specific subsets of patients living with breast cancer (Gao et al, 2018).

By pooling this data, the FDA can identify trends that may not be significant in individual trials alone.

Cancer centers and pharma companies are integrating similarly diverse data to improve their understanding of drug performance in the clinic. One of our biopharmaceutical partners, Sanofi, leverages RWE data, such as anonymized health insurance claims and medical records, in combination with AI techniques to characterize the response of well-defined patient populations to their drugs.

“All these [data types] work in specific databases, in a very independent way. To be able to do analysis using pre-clinical, real world, and clinical data, technically was not possible until we had tools similar to the ones that Palantir offers. We see a great value in the integration of all this data to solve problems we couldn’t otherwise solve,” said Dr. Bernard Hamelin, Global Head of Medical Evidence Generation at Sanofi.

Starting with a cohort of 17 million patients with ~2,500 characteristics and 7-year medical histories, Dr. Hamelin and his team identified 60 patient clusters of interest. For these clusters, they identified 5 new indications, with an understanding of drug response for each group. It took the team only ten weeks to identify indications that would otherwise be missed because of constraints in scale and data complexity.

At a large, NCI-designated cancer center, scientists struggled to manage heterogeneity across patients. When a group of patients walked in with the same diagnosis, the same treatment produced different outcomes.

The cancer center used Foundry to integrate data from electronic medical records, lab tests, and genomic panels, as well as public sources, to represent the diverse range of patient journeys through the health system. The center also recently launched an initiative to standardize the collection of a series of biopsies from patients over the course of their treatment journey, and worked with us to organize that data in a way that would support decision-making and clinical research.

Listening to our partners and hearing the new questions asked, avenues of collaboration opened, and results delivered, I have renewed conviction in our commitment to continuously solving the general data integration problem. By removing the barriers between the growing universe of biomedical data and the subject matter experts best positioned to use that data, we can tangibly improve the lives of patients.

I can’t wait to see where we are at HealthCon 2020!
