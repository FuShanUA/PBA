A Sky Full of Clouds

Robert Fink
14 min read
·
Apr 27, 2020

Palantir’s customers use our Foundry and Gotham software platforms to solve their hardest data problems. Many of our customers work in highly-regulated environments, which introduces additional constraints on how we can operate our software. This article presents the complexities of running SaaS platforms in heterogeneous environments and sketches the solutions we have come up with to manage our hundreds of dedicated installations across the fleet.

One of the most common misconceptions about our software is that “Palantir sucks up all data into its database”, implying that we maintain some form of omniscient data vacuum cleaner that gobbles up data from all of our customers into a central place. Quite the opposite is true, though: we bring our software to our customers, rather than their data to our software. Each customer manages their data in Foundry and Gotham environments; these environments can be physically separated — for instance, in the case of disconnected installations for government agencies — or separated into virtual environments by means of networking, encryption, or access control boundaries. Of course, our customers also maintain full legal ownership of their data.

Consequently, our software engineers have to maintain a large number of Foundry or Gotham installations in a variety of different deployment environments. Every installation comprises hundreds of microservices, frontend applications, and database systems, developed by dozens of engineering teams across the world. We like to say that we maintain a sky full of clouds, in the sense that every installation itself is a complex cloud-native application of which we maintain several hundreds. Those “clouds” come in all sorts of shapes: we run our software on a variety of commercial cloud providers, and some of our government customers have legal constraints that require installations in classified data centers without Internet connectivity.

While both Foundry and Gotham use a Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) model, we have developed several options beyond what you might find at a typical SaaS provider to meet the unique requirements of our customers. One configuration is our Dedicated Foundry and Dedicated Gotham offering, which allow Palantir to meet our customers’ specific data residency and isolation requirements. While still a part of our fully-managed cloud platform, Dedicated Foundry and Dedicated Gotham represent a partitioning of our SaaS platform into additional installations.

To cope with this complexity, we have developed a range of deployment infrastructure tools. The remainder of this post describes the different deployment scenarios that we support, our continuous delivery systems (from a 10,000-foot point of view), and finally the tools we provide to our developers for monitoring, debugging, and tuning their applications.

Deployment Scenarios

We work with customers all over the world that have a wide range of compliance, business, and security considerations. Data localisation laws may dictate that customer data is stored in specific geographic regions, e.g., in a specific country or in the European Union. Other customers may have strong preferences for or against certain cloud providers, typically for architectural or business reasons. Some of our government customers around the world use our software to manage data in classified environments, often in physically separate data centers and networks without Internet connectivity.

Production systems in large organizations typically require process and change management accreditations that encode best practices for developing, maintaining, and documenting software systems. For example, most US government agencies require software to be hosted in a FedRAMP accredited environment. The majority of our customers expect SOC or ISO 27001 certifications.

We’ve offered Foundry and Gotham as a SaaS model via public cloud providers since 2014, and today the vast majority of installations are hosted in AWS or Azure. We have recently become FedRAMP Moderate and DoD Impact Level 5 accredited in order to support our cloud-based SaaS offering for US government customers and are pursuing similar accreditations in other legislations, for instance IRAP in Australia for PROTECTED data.

Skylab: Managing individual environments

To meet the requirements and expectations of our customers, we had to invest in deployment infrastructure that works in the complex set of scenarios described above. To manage the hundreds of services, applications, and database systems running within a single Foundry or Gotham installation, we first built a system called Skylab (named after the United States’ first Space Station). Skylab is responsible for the life cycle (e.g., install, start, stop, upgrade) of each application as well as for their configuration (RPC and service discovery, encryption keys, and other runtime configuration).

Administrators configure applications through a Web UI, and Skylab takes care of performing the necessary configuration or life cycle changes to the affected machines and applications.

Internally, Skylab follows a declarative model similar to Kubernetes: administrators set the expected final state, and Skylab executes the steps required to take the application from the current to the expected state. Each machine in an environment runs a so-called deployment agent that executes life cycle commands relayed from the central Skylab control plane.

Applications managed by Skylab must adhere to our service layout specification (SLS) that defines how to bundle application code, life cycle management scripts (start/stop/restart), hardware requirements (e.g., memory, disk, CPU), and default configuration into a self-contained tarball. For example, in order to upgrade an application, Skylab will download the new application SLS package from an artifact repository, copy it to the target machine, and then ask the deployment agent to stop the old application process, apply configuration overrides to the default configuration, and start the new version of the application.

To summarize, Skylab is the control plane for all Foundry or Gotham components within a single installation.

Apollo: Managing many environments

While Skylab simplified the maintenance of the hundreds of components in a single Foundry or Gotham installation, it quickly became clear that Skylab alone is still an ineffective tool for maintaining the hundreds of Foundry and Gotham installations:

  • First, Skylab requires administrators to perform manual tasks to upgrade or configure a given application; this is time-consuming and error-prone.
  • Second, our installations quickly diverged in terms of application versions and configuration, making debugging and performance analysis much more difficult for our developers.

We next built Apollo, a continuous delivery system for Foundry and Gotham, with two primary goals in mind:

  • First, to harmonize and automate installation management across installations.
  • Second, as an enabler for safe software delivery and dramatically improved feedback loops for our developers.

Apollo acts as a puppet master for the Skylab instances in the different installations: instead of requiring administrators to perform the same upgrade or configuration tasks in each of the environments, we now declare software version and configuration targets centrally in Apollo’s configuration repository and let Apollo propagate the necessary changes to the relevant Skylab instances.

Conceptually, Apollo is very simple: each of the Skylab instances continually submits the current state of its environment e.g., which applications are running and in which version, the health of each application, the current configuration, etc. Apollo compares the observed current state to the declared target state and computes a queue of change tasks to be applied for each of the environments; Skylab instances fetch change tasks from the queue and actuate the changes via their respective deployment agents. Like Skylab, Apollo has a Web UI through which administrators and developers see which component is installed in which environment, observe upgrade progress, or recall releases (see below).

The automation provided by Apollo allows us to do a number of cool things, for example:

Continuous delivery. Our build systems publish Foundry and Gotham components to a central artifact repository that is connected to Apollo. Apollo automatically learns which versions of which components are available and applies upgrades subject to certain rules; for instance, major upgrades may require longer soak times (see below), while patches or bug fixes can be rolled out more quickly.

Safe delivery. Apollo is a critical component in our software quality and testing methodology: we use pre-merge unit tests as well as synthetic integration or end-to-end tests (executed in our continuous integration environments), and then aggressively upgrade canary environments with new releases.

Many components release to canary environments directly from the develop branch, resulting in a dramatically shortened feedback loop for our software developers: instead of waiting weeks or months to install a release to production, it is now common practice to observe new features or configuration changes in canary installations (e.g., our internal Foundry installations that run Palantir’s own data analytics and workflows) within minutes.

Apollo automatically adjudicates releases by observing performance metrics and error states, and then gradually rolls out releases that pass adjudication to the fleet, starting with internal installations and eventually reaching more conservative, mission-critical environments. Services and environments can indicate their risk appetite and thus choose a suitable trade-off between “fast but risky” or “slow but tested” upgrades.

Dependency resolution. Each Foundry and Gotham component specifies its dependencies — including the component name and compatible version range — on other components. (This specification is part of the SLS package mentioned above.) Apollo uses the dependency graph to order upgrades in terms of their compatibility.

Further, Apollo understands which releases require database migrations that constitute upgrade or downgrade barriers and chooses upgrade paths compatible with such constraints; for example, Apollo will upgrade a given service through each of a sequence of mandatory database migrations instead of simply fast-tracking to the latest available release that would leave the database in an incompatible state.

This means that even complex upgrades and migrations are hands-off for developers: they merely indicate the presence of a database migration in their release metadata and Apollo takes care of rolling out the release in a safe way.

Recalling. If a particular release of an application has been identified as buggy, unstable, or slow, we can recall the release and force the upgrade or downgrade to a known good version across all environments. Such change tasks due to recall events take precedence over regular continuous delivery upgrades so that critical bug fixes get rolled out quickly.

Recalls can be done by administrators and developers, or by Apollo itself: Apollo adjudication system monitors the health of each component over time (for instance by observing error logs and performance metrics) and automatically recalls releases that appear unhealthy.

Blue/green deployment. This is a blog post in itself, but in a nutshell the idea of blue/green deployment is to upgrade an application by installing a second instance of an application and slowly shifting traffic from the old instance to the new one.

All of our applications and services can be deployed in highly-available, multi-node configurations that support zero-downtime upgrades. In such highly-available setups, we can simply add and remove nodes one by one in order to upgrade from old to new version.

Apollo’s release adjudication mechanism (see above) starts collecting performance and error signal as soon as the first node has been upgraded, yielding early indicators for the quality of a release. In this sense, blue/green deployment is not only a zero-downtime rollout mechanic, but also an extra safety net that allows us to further accelerate the feedback loop for developers.

Soak testing. Apollo examines the structured change logs of each release in order to assess its risk; for example, minor cosmetic changes or bug fixes can be considered lower risk while API changes, new features, or database migrations often indicate higher risk releases. Apollo adjusts the soak time, i.e., the amount of time spent observing and adjudicating a release before rolling it out more broadly, according to these risk heuristics.

Change management policies. Apollo’s declarative state (e.g., which applications should be installed with what configuration) is stored in a Git repository. We use a rich system of merge and code review policies to control who can perform changes to an installation. For example, we can enforce segregation of duties by requiring distinct approvals from developers on separate teams before code changes will merge.

To summarize, Apollo is a continuous delivery system that manages the entire fleet of Palantir’s Foundry and Gotham installations. It works by forwarding configuration changes and software upgrade tasks to the respective per-environment Skylab instances.

Asynchronous Delivery

While Apollo as described above works well for installations running in cloud environments, it doesn’t solve the problem for the government customers who must operate in disconnected networks. These environments have one-way, high-latency communication: shipping new software releases into these environments often involves burning DVDs, virus scanners, and humans uploading binaries to servers.

Side note: This configuration is only available to government customers as part of our classified offerings. The reasoning behind this decision is simple: physically disconnected environments are lose-lose, they are vastly more expensive for the customer, more difficult to manage, and harder to keep up-to-date. In fact, for organizations whose networks are not air-gapped, an on-premise environment may be more of a security risk. For example, while a one-week delay for a critical security patch may be a calculated, acceptable risk for a disconnected installation on a classified government network (because there is no cable connection to the Internet and there are physical boundaries between the installation and its adversaries), such a delay is not acceptable for our commercial customers with Internet-connected systems. Apollo and continuous delivery allow us to roll out such fixes across all connected installations with minimal delay.

Our first approach to continuous asynchronous delivery was driven by the Forward Deployed Engineering teams (i.e., the software engineers working directly with the respective customers) responsible for managing disconnected installations. Inspired by the step-change improvements that the Apollo brought to our connected cloud environments, they built a slimmed down version of Apollo, named SLIM, that could be run in each environment.

For each of these disconnected environments, we ran a replica of the environment internally where we could stage upgrades of each of the components. We first validated the set of versions we wanted to upgrade, then snapshotted the application versions and configuration, and finally shipped the configuration snapshot together with the relevant release binaries to the customer environment. After the Forward Deployed Engineering team got the snapshot into the environment, Skylab and SLIM in concert would actuate the changes described by the snapshot.

This approach served us well initially, but as Apollo developed more advanced capabilities, such as blue/green deployment, we found that we had to invest a lot in SLIM to keep feature parity with Apollo and we faced some tough decisions around how to handle some of Apollo’s capabilities, like automated release recalls, that did not make sense to run in disconnected environments because they relied on release feedback from multiple installations.

To simplify our processes, we decided to refactor Apollo so that we could deploy stand-alone Apollo installations in all disconnected environments. (At this point we also decided that Apollo should be able to continuously upgrade itself.)

Coincidentally, our FedRAMP architecture relies on a dedicated Apollo instance that manages the Foundry and Gotham installations in FedRAMP cloud environments; the refactoring effort thus enabled both the disconnected and the FedRAMP workflows. Today, we use the same roll-out and recall strategies in our disconnected and our cloud environments to ensure that our software has predictable, stable configurations across all of our installations.

Collecting Signal

As we all know, software is bound to behave in exciting and unexpected ways, so it is important that Foundry and Gotham are built in a way that allows developers to understand and debug their components even if they don’t have access to the installed software or machines. To this end, we rely on two main sources of debug signal: performance metrics plus system and software logs.

The tricky bit is deciding which metrics and logs can be collected and provided to developers without any risk of leaking sensitive customer information. The base rule is that no customer data and no user-generated content may leave the environment. Conservatively (we are rather safe than sorry), this means that developers can only rely on compile-time static data (e.g., code constants) or random identifiers for their debug workflows. To split sensitive information from what we call “safe” (in the sense of “safe to log”) information, we invested in logging infrastructure and libraries that allow developers to tag certain information as “safe”, for example:

log.error("Failed to read dataset",
    SafeArg.of("datasetId", datasetId),
    UnsafeArg.of("userId", userId));

The logging infrastructure can differentiate between safe and unsafe arguments and only provide the safe information to developers. In this case, the developer would have access to the error message and the random dataset identifier, but not the the possibly sensitive user identifier. We use compile-time code analysis to verify that developers do not accidentally leak unsafe information, for instance via:

log.error("Failed to read dataset, user=" + userId);

This code does not compile on our continuous integration infrastructure and thus cannot make it into a release. Separating safe from unsafe log information gives our engineers maximal information while ensuring we never unintentionally expose customer data to developers.

All of our services emit performance and health metrics in a structured format that Apollo can inspect, for instance in order to compare the performance of two software versions during a blue/green upgrade.

In addition to service logs, RPC request logs, and performance metrics, our deployment infrastructure also supports automated collection of events, distributed stack traces (similar to Zipkin and OpenTracing), thread dumps and diagnostic logs such as garbage collection statistics. We collect more than 30TB of logs daily and keep logs for debugging workflows for 14 days. Our endpoint metrics encompass approximately 80 million unique time series and 100 billion data points every day.

You can read more about our telemetry infrastructure in two previous blog posts on log parsing and indexing.

Conclusion

Skylab and Apollo have reached a level of maturity that allow us to operate hundreds of installations of hundreds of services and database systems (aka, “a sky full of clouds”) in a mostly automated fashion. Our deployment infrastructure is flexible enough to allow installations in a variety of different environments (AWS, Azure, and classified environments), and mature enough to be certified under strict process management regimes such as FedRAMP or SOC2.

We hope you enjoyed this whirlwind tour of our production infrastructure. If you want to learn more, we encourage you to check out our blog posts or conference talks on Rubix, our secure multi-tenant Kubernetes infrastructure for data processing workloads. (Rubix installations are also managed by Apollo, of course!)
