Editor’s note: This is the last post in our blog series “How we do Agile”. Part 1, Part 2, Part 3, Part 4 (this post). Caitlin has worked with fellow Palantirians and a customer team on building the largest data asset in the aviation industry. In this post she sheds some light on the collaboration culture and processes in her super-heterogeneous team.

For the past two years, a Palantir team has been part of a joint effort in the aviation industry — specifically, models for real-world objects (like airplanes themselves, their construction schedules and maintenance programmes, etc.), pipelines, and infrastructure to scale across the industry. The combined team — with Palantir Deployment Strategists and software developers, a number of customer product and business owners, and third-party developers — spans four companies, nine time zones, and more than ten native languages. This is the story of how, with the help of the Agile framework, we evolved into working — mostly! — as one team.

From the start of the project both the Palantir and customer teams were operating in their own Agile environments. Components of Agile philosophy are central to how Palantirian teams typically operate (see Carlene’s earlier post in this series), and the customer had invested heavily in a SAFE (Scaled Agile For Enterprises) program to synchronize business objectives across multiple teams. Yet, we were essentially two Agile teams with related but distinct goals, geographies, languages, timelines, and priorities, trying to work in concert.

The Agile manifesto does not prescribe a single “right” way to do Agile, does not mandate particular processes or tools. Instead it offers 4 key values and 12 guiding principles to help teams find their stride. Over the course of several months, we — product owners, scrum masters, Agile coaches, business owners, and developers — tried different variants of Agile to hone in our what works best for our teams. In finding our rhythm, three Agile ideals were especially important:

  • Respond to change over following a plan
  • Reflection at regular intervals
  • Individuals and interactions over processes & tools

The remainder of this post showcases how our joint Palantir/customer team interpreted these principles.

Responding to change over following a plan

Agile is simply a philosophy, intended to empower teams to figure out how to work well together. This means, once you realize something’s not working, change it.

Being Agile means trying different approaches and figuring out what interactions work best for your team, at a given time. Conditions within the team will fluctuate as the focus shifts, the type of work changes, and members move in and out of the team. Over the past six months, we’ve had success with Agile because we continued to try new approaches that we think might foster a more productive, collaborative team. Sometimes our hypothesis is wrong, but we’re happy to try, and fail, quickly. For example, we needed a better way to track shared issues across teams; each team had a well-established system for tracking their own work, but tool silos inhibited collaboration on dependencies. We thought a shared sprint board might close this gap, but after a short-lived experiment, realized an additional tool ultimately created more confusion. We reverted to each team using their individual processes, with live discussions to synchronize on common activities. This afforded continued flexibility for each smaller team, one of which eventually pivoted away from electronic tracking tools in favor of physical post-its.

Reflection at regular intervals

Like with any skill you want to improve, for a team to work better together they need to take stock of their current working style to figure out what’s going well and where they’re falling short. Sounds easy enough, but honest self-reflection is not so simple. Agile teams need to be democratic with all team members feeling psychologically safe to speak their mind, otherwise you risk getting biased feedback from only certain individuals or personality types, and you likely miss out on the best ideas! (This is obviously not news to anyone familiar with the research of Amy Edmondson, Google, or Edgar Schein.)

Two easy and powerful ways to get regular feedback from the full team are, in my experience:

  • Create open/safe feedback channels. If you have a few dominating personalities, try using non-verbal feedback systems (e.g. write comments on post-its) and strongly encourage feedback from everyone, for instance by allocating speaking time slots.
  • Get an outsider’s perspective on how the team interacts. We occasionally invite Agile coaches to join our demos or retros and then get their feedback on the spot at the end of the meeting. They have pointed out things like, “your retro elicits honest feedback, but what are your one to two tangible action items for your team to improve?”

With reflection, our team realized that some team members lacked context of their teammates’ work, and if they didn’t feel comfortable asking questions, the knowledge gap continued to expand even further. We stopped dividing into smaller groups during quarterly planning so team members acquired a baseline understanding of all user stories. And, given we are a large team with broad scope, we focused on sharing the business context and value during demos.

On a macro level, we also have quarterly workshops with key players from all teams — Palantir, product owners, business owners, and developers. These sessions always start with a retrospective to uncover friction, discuss how we’ve progressed since the last meeting, and articulate what is working well and what is not. This sets a workshop tone of openness and transparency, and in part helps us refine the workshop’s agenda.

Individuals and interactions over processes and tools

If you’ve ever worked with a remote team, you know there’s no substitute to being in the same location, at least occasionally. For us, this feeling was compounded because we had two remote teams with only sporadic colocation.

We needed someone to bridge the relationship between the teams, to make sure that each team was aware of the other’s deadlines and dependencies. To address this, a Deployment Strategist worked onsite at the customer’s main office. By having this point person, we saw a step-jump in communication between the teams, in particular as compared to previous efforts that relied on electronic tools like JIRA and Slack. We could identify and raise blockers across teams, and make changes to empower self-organized teams. A prime example: Palantir developers often support customer or third-party developers, for instance through pair programming or code reviews. To remove dependencies on Palantir code reviews and create a more autonomous team, we helped level up a handful of strong developers on the customer team to be internal reviewers.

We tried many other things to make our team feel more cohesive — a fixed daily time slot for stand ups, planning and retro meetings for all customer and third-party developers, or pairing folks from different offices to cross-pollinate skills — but ultimately it’s tricky to collaborate reactively with a 6- to 12-hour time zone difference. We ended up gravitating towards working with team members in our immediate time zone (in our case, it became one team in Europe and one in Asia). Not surprisingly, colocated teams sped up iterations for day-to-day work, and we still have the larger team for knowledge sharing and unblocking. When it works, we plan and tackle projects across timezones, but we don’t force ourselves into this modus operandi.

Conclusion

There’s no perfect way to implement Agile. In fact, if you are fixated on how to do Agile correctly, take a step back. Don’t ask “are we doing Agile right?”, but “What is the right interpretation of Agile for us?” By definition, Agile requires continuous iteration, so don’t be afraid to try and fail. As long as you keep trying.
